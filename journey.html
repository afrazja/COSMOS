Era I — The Race
1957

Sputnik 1

A polished 58-centimeter sphere with four antennas becomes the first artificial satellite. Its steady radio beep — audible to amateur operators around the world — announces the Space Age to everyone with a receiver.

83.6 kg · orbited Earth ~1,440 times
The full story

Sputnik was almost an afterthought — a simple "PS-1" sphere rushed to the pad when the fancier science satellite fell behind schedule. Its rocket, the R-7, was designed to carry warheads; its designers dreamed of carrying people.

The beep changed everything. Within a year the United States had created NASA, poured money into science education, and the Space Race was on. One small sphere, three months in orbit, and history split into before and after.

1961

Gagarin flies

Yuri Gagarin becomes the first human being in space, circling the planet once aboard Vostok 1. From launch to landing, the flight that changed our species' address lasted less than two hours.

108 minutes · 1 orbit · age 27
The full story

Gagarin was chosen partly for his calm and partly, famously, for his smile. The flight was almost fully automatic — engineers weren’t sure a human could function in weightlessness, so the manual controls were locked behind a coded envelope.

He ejected as planned at 7 km and parachuted into a potato field, where a startled farmer and her granddaughter met a man in an orange suit who said: "Don’t be afraid — I am a Soviet like you, and I must find a telephone to call Moscow."

1969

Boots on the Moon

Apollo 11's Eagle lands with about 25 seconds of fuel to spare. Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin walk on another world while an estimated 600 million people — a fifth of humanity — watch live.

21.5 hrs on the surface · 21.6 kg of Moon brought home
The full story

The landing nearly didn’t happen: the computer flashed 1202 alarms (it was overloaded, but safely so — the software Margaret Hamilton’s team wrote shed low-priority tasks and kept flying), and Armstrong took manual control to fly past a boulder field with ~25 seconds of fuel left.

The plaque on the ladder reads: "We came in peace for all mankind." Five more crews landed after them; the last footprints, from 1972, are still there — no wind to erase them.

Era II — The Explorers
1977

The Voyagers depart

Twin probes launch on a once-in-176-years planetary alignment, cameras first at Jupiter and Saturn, then beyond. Voyager 1 is now the most distant human-made object — and it is still whispering data home.

25+ billion km away · carries the Golden Record
The full story

The Voyagers found worlds stranger than anyone imagined: volcanoes erupting on Io, a cracked ice shell on Europa, nitrogen geysers on Triton — every flyby rewrote the textbooks in a night.

Each carries a Golden Record: greetings in 55 languages, whale song, Chuck Berry, a heartbeat. Voyager 1 crossed into interstellar space in 2012 and still calls home daily — on a transmitter using less power than a refrigerator lightbulb.

1990

Hubble opens its eye

Above the blur of the atmosphere, the Hubble Space Telescope begins rewriting textbooks: the universe's age, black holes at galactic centers, and one famous long stare at "empty" sky that revealed thousands of galaxies.

1.6+ million observations and counting
The full story

Hubble launched with a flawed mirror — ground perfectly, to the wrong shape, off by 1/50th the width of a human hair. The world called it a national embarrassment.

Then, in 1993, seven astronauts caught it in orbit and fitted corrective optics across five spacewalks — glasses for a telescope. It became the most productive scientific instrument in history, and it fixed something bigger: the idea that big failures can be repaired.

1997

First wheels on Mars

The microwave-oven-sized Sojourner rover rolls off its lander and becomes the first vehicle to drive on another planet — the great-grandmother of every Mars rover since.

11.5 kg · drove ~100 meters
The full story

Pathfinder landed inside giant airbags — it hit Mars at 50 km/h and bounced 15 times before rolling to a stop. Inside was Sojourner, named by a 12-year-old girl who won a student essay contest, after abolitionist Sojourner Truth.

Designed to last a week, it worked for almost three months, and the mission’s website became one of the first global internet sensations — proof the public would show up for Mars.

1998

The Station rises

The first module of the International Space Station launches. Since November 2000 there has not been a single day with every human on Earth — someone has always been living in orbit.

continuously crewed for 25 years · 16 sunrises a day
The full story

The ISS is the most expensive object ever built — assembled over 40+ launches by 15 countries whose engineers didn’t even share a language, docking modules that had never touched each other until they met in orbit, where they fit.

Its real product is knowledge you can’t get on Earth: what a year in weightlessness does to a body, how flames and crystals and immune systems behave without gravity — the operating manual for the voyages to come.

Era III — The New Age
2012

Seven minutes of terror

Too heavy for airbags, the car-sized Curiosity rover is lowered onto Mars by a hovering rocket crane — a landing so audacious its own engineers nicknamed the descent "seven minutes of terror." It worked, flawlessly.

899 kg · still exploring Gale Crater today
The full story

Curiosity was too heavy for airbags and too delicate for legs alone — so engineers invented the sky crane: a hovering rocket platform that lowered the rover on cables, then flew away to crash at a safe distance. It had to work perfectly, on the first try, with no human in the loop. It did.

The gamble paid off scientifically too: Curiosity found an ancient freshwater lakebed with carbon chemistry — proof that Mars was once the kind of place where life could have made a living.

2015

Pluto, at last

After a nine-and-a-half-year sprint, New Horizons races past Pluto and finds not a dead rock but a world with nitrogen glaciers and a heart-shaped plain — completing the first reconnaissance of the classical solar system.

9.5 years · 5 billion km · flyby at 49,600 km/h
The full story

New Horizons left Earth faster than any spacecraft before it, crossing the Moon’s distance in nine hours. It slept through most of the decade-long cruise to save wear, waking yearly for checkups.

Pluto was supposed to be a dead ice ball. Instead: nitrogen glaciers flowing today, blue skies, and a heart-shaped plain now named Tombaugh Regio, after the Kansas farm boy who discovered Pluto in 1930.

2020

Spaceflight goes commercial

A privately built spacecraft — SpaceX's Crew Dragon — carries astronauts to orbit for the first time. Rockets that land themselves and fly again stop being science fiction and start being Tuesday.

first crewed commercial flight · boosters reused 20+ times
The full story

After the Shuttle retired in 2011, America spent nine years buying seats on Russian rockets. NASA’s bet — pay companies for the service, not the vehicle — was controversial until the moment Bob Behnken and Doug Hurley splashed down safely.

The deeper shift was economic: boosters that land and fly again cut the cost of reaching orbit several-fold. Cheap access is the quiet revolution every future mission stands on.

2021

New eyes, new wings

The James Webb Space Telescope launches to see the universe's first galaxies in infrared — while on Mars, the little helicopter Ingenuity makes the first powered flight on another world: a 39-second Wright-brothers moment, 280 million kilometers from Kitty Hawk.

JWST: 6.5 m mirror · Ingenuity: 72 flights
The full story

Webb had 344 single-point failures — 344 chances for one jammed bolt to end a $10 billion, 25-year effort — and had to unfold alone, a million and a half kilometers from any help. Every single step worked.

That same spring, on Mars, a 1.8-kg helicopter named Ingenuity attempted the first powered flight on another world in air 1% as thick as Earth’s. Planned for 5 flights, it flew 72. Somewhere between those two machines is the lesson: careful and audacious both work.

Era IV — What's Next
Soon

The return — and the leap

Artemis aims to put the first woman on the Moon and build a permanent presence there. Starship is being built to carry a hundred people at a time. And somewhere in a classroom right now sits the first human who will walk on Mars.
